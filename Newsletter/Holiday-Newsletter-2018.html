San Pedro is located at the far north edge of the Salar (Salt Flats) of the Atacama desert, one of the driest places in the world at an elevation of 7,900 ft. above sea level. Although quite high, the town is near the foothill of the Andes which rise all the way to 19,000 ft. at the top of Licancabur volcano nearby.

There are vast areas at high elevation that constitute a separate ecosystem and are generally known as the altiplano (highlands). Here is Laguna Miscanti at 13,000 ft. elevation.

The altiplano is a harsh region with a limited flora and fauna adapted to the thin air and harsh conditions. For example, the vicuña is plentiful in this area.

Finally, it was time to return to Carlsbad and say good bye to Chile until next year.

The mountains in the Bolivian cordillera are covered with mineral layers that give it amazing colors. The vegetation is nearly nonexistent above 15,000 feet.

This is the hotel we stayed in the mountains of Bolivia. The landscape is otherwordly. It looks more like the surface of Mars than anything else. The hotel is located at 14,800 ft. elevation and is the only building within a 50 mile radius. It is completely self-sufficient, relying on wind turbines, solar power, and generators. Given the harsh conditions and isolation, I was pleasantly surprised by the excellent quality of the food and great service.

Eventually, we began to descend towards a large flat expanse. The flattest point on earth, Salar the Uyuni.

If the mountains in the Bolivian altiplano seemed out of this world, the salt flats are down right surreal. We lucked out because we had some rain the night before our visit which turns the entire expanse of the salt flats into a gigantic mirror.

The landscape is unbelievably beautiful. Also, the temperature was quite a bit higher than in the mountains, after all we were “only” at 12,000 feet of elevation.
