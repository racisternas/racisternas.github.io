Deadvlei is a small area of Sossusvlei National Park where a grove of dead trees still stands after 1,000 years. The trees were killed when a shift in the sand dunes closed off the river that fed their small valley. The dead trees stand on a layer of salt and are preserved by the dry weather. Their small valley of Deadvlei is surrounded on all sides by giant red sand dunes. Notice in the two pictures above and to the left how the passage of time quickly changes the light conditions and dramatically alters the perceived colors of the landscape.

My first stop in Morocco was Casablanca a city of 3.2 million people on the Atlantic Coast.

Indisputably, the most beautiful building in the city of Casablanca is the Hassan II mosque built in the mid-1990s on the shores of the Atlantic Ocean. In fact, part of the building is resting on columns that are placed in the waters. This is a reference to a Quranic verse that says that God's throne rests upon the waters.

It is an enormous building that can house 30,000 people and is sorrounded by a large esplanade where an additional 70,000 can join for prayers during major holidays.

The interior of the mosque was entirely built by local artisans using domestic materials in the late 1990s. The different types of stone have beautiful colors and the carvings are beautifully proportioned and exquisitely detailed.

At night, the mosque is illuminated throughout. There is a green laser beam at the top of the minaret (see left) that shines its light in the direction of Mecca so the faithful can know in which direction to pray.

What do you do after a two month trip through six countries in two continents? You unpack your bags and begin processing the 8,000 pictures I took. Also, I began to plan my next adventure: a road trip to Banff National Park in Alberta, Canada in September.

Driving to Canada is very, very easy. All you need is a good car and a lot of patience along the long road. To break down the road trip time, I planned on visiting several interesting places along the way.

My first stop was Salt Lake City. I was not really interested in the town itself but on Antelope Island State Park nearby. It is home to a large herd of Bison which wander all over the island frequently crossing the roads. That makes them easy to see up close. Bisons were almost driven to extinction but now there are several herds of bison mostly on government lands in the western states.

The next stop was Craters of the Moon National Monument in Idaho. It is a large area covered in lava fields, cinder cones, lava tubes, etc. It is a very similar experience to visiting the Hawaiian lava fields

The main area in the northern district of the park is called Mammoth Hot Springs. It is an extraordinary place where the hot water and minerals feed a population of different colorful thermophile bacteria which cover the hills with strange swirling patterns. The minerals also form beautiful layered pools of water.

This is the view of the small valley where Mammoth Hot Springs is located. The white buildings on the left and center belong to the hotel and the building with the red roof is part of the original Fort Yellowstone and now the park visitor center.

Why a fort? Yellowstone National Park was an experiment. It was the first National Park in the United States and the initial attempts to keep vandals and thieves out of the federal lands with a small number of armed employees proved insufficient so at the turn of the century the army stationed some troops that built barracks and the fort building. The army kept the peace until the U.S. Park Rangers were established for that purpose.

Yellowstone national Park is enormous. My main regret from my road trips is not being able to spend more time at each stop. I only spent two nights in Yellowstone and there is enough land, mountains, lakes, rivers, trails, geysers and animals to spend easily a whole week.
