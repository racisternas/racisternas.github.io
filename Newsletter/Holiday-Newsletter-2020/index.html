Through the years, I have been writing an end-of-year newsletter to reach out to all of you and say: "I am thinking about you even though we may not talk often". I try to use photography to illustrate what has caught my eye in the preceding twelve months and to share those experiences with you.

2020 was different than other years.

Happy Holidays!

A big part of the year was spent indoors while human contact was reduced to cautious hellos from a distance. Food shopping became a complicated adventure where toilet paper and cleaning supplies became cherished trophies. There were no movies, theater, or other live performances and no travel opportunities. At the same time, there was plenty of room for introspection or the pursuit of books, music, and podcasts. A great time to find streaming sites for foreign films. Hiking, at a safe distance, was an option.

Quietness painted our solitude.

It would have been a great vacation of the mind except for the endless stream of terrible news. People getting sick, some of them seriously ill, some dying. People losing their livelihoods and growing desperate.

Meanwhile, we also witnessed innumerable acts of courage, large and small, carried out by first responders, ER nurses, and all types of essential workers williing to risk their health to keep our complex work in motion in spite of the pandemic and the lockdown.

There was all that and so much more including protests, a nauseating presidential election cycle, wild fires, floods, locusts... well, maybe not locusts but 2020 was a smorgasbord of unpleasantness.

My year started with a repeat performance from previous years.

My refrigerator leaked while I visited Chile and I came back from my trip to find my kitchen floor and walls damaged by another home flood. The fourth one in 8 years. Maybe some divine punishment?

Soon, I was caught in a frenzy of activities involving my home insurance, contractors, shopping for materials, scheduling painters, planning to vacate my home while the floor was torn up, looking for a place to stay. But not all was bad news at that point. I had previously planned a blitz of a vacation at the end of January (which unbeknownst to me, would be the last time I would leave town for a while).

The last week of January, I headed to San Diego airport and boarded a flight to Prague, a city I had always longed to visit. Wandering around the city is a lot of fun. The buildings in the historic core of Prague are beautiful and very well kept and the city is full of works of art on the streets.

Prague is a couple of hours by train from Dresden and Vienna, so I took the opportunity to also visit those two cities as day trips. Dresden was burned to the ground during WWII by allied bombers and it took decades to complete the rebuilding of its historic core but the final result is wonderful.

The Fürstenzug is a ceramic tile mural depicting all the rulers of Saxony over an 800 year period ending in 1904

A clueless tourist vainly attempting to stay dry.

The Zwinger. A beautiful baroque building with gardens.

Vienna is a fantastic place, which deserves more than a day trip. Unfortunaltely, that is all the time I had so the choice was clear: try to walk around as much of the historic city core as possible.

The first stop from the train station is the Belvedere palace which houses a huge art collection of master painters form the middle ages to the 20th century. Below is Klimt's Kiss.

Jacques-Louis David's  "Napoleon Crossing the Alps"

The Karlskirche, built by the Habsburgs in the 18th century. The ornate columns with the bas reliefs and the golden imperial eagles at the top are there to intimidate you if you were an imperial subject.

The frescoes in the ceiling are amazing

The main nave and the altar of the cathedral. This was proof that the power of the Austro Hungarian empire came straight from God himself.

Being lunchtime, I headed for the Naschmarkt, an open air market full of restaurants. With the weather being balmy, it was the perfect place to eat outdoors.

The St. Stephen's Cathedral is one of the tallest buildings in Vienna. It was built during the middle ages.

The Vienna Rathouse (city hall, not a house for rats). It has a huge skating rink in set up in the park in front during winter.

This is the famous Hofburg palace that houses the Spanish Riding School where the beautiful Lipizzaner stallions perform.

The Vienna State Opera House.

All vacations must come to an end some time so here I was back in Carlsbad hearing about the spread of COVID and getting ready to tear apart my house to replace the floorboards. I was generously invited by my friends Steve and Jeane to stay with them while all the work took place. They live three miles from my house so I could visit each day and see the demolition and reconstruction as it developed.

Of course, I could go on and on and show you 500 more pictures until your mouse finger begged for mercy but what would be the point? All three cities are wonderful destinations and I will probably visit them again some day after the pandemic is over... speaking of pandemic...

In the end, everything worked out. No, Jack did not invite me to take a long walk down a short pier and yes, the new floors and everything else in the house looked terrific. There was just that teensy little issue that I was home bound during a pandemic with nothing to do and nowhere to go.

I had a place to stay, a plan, a budget, fully competent workers and if that were not all, I had an ace in the hole: my friend Jack who has overseen multiple projects in my home previously. Just look at that face. It has serene confidence (and slipping schedules) written all over it.

I mean, what could possibly go wrong, am I right?

Well, before I start screaming like a South Park character, I will tell you what could go wrong. Remember COVID? well, by early March we had lockdowns, and gym closures, and oh, I work for 24 hour Fitness which suddenly had to close all its gyms so suddenly I was on furlough getting by on unemployment checks courtesy of the State of California which incidentaly, also meant I did not have enough money to pay for the beautiful new floors and painted walls.

I know that most of you were dealing with the same issues in 2020. In my case, it seemed that the year was moving along at full speed and then everything stopped dead on its tracks. With nowhere to go, and no job, things became a lot more boring around here. That also meant fewer pictures to share. But, this being San Diego county, you can always count on the great outdoors to provide a distraction so...

In April, I visited the Carlsbad rose garden to enjoy the sheer variety of rose bushes in bloom.

COVID took away most Halloween celebrations from our neighborhood. Fortunately, spiders did not notice and they were still busily weaving their webs as usual. I found this beautiful specimen of orb weaver in my next-door neighbors front yard.

During May, we had a major red tide event on the coast of San Diego county. This caused a huge bioluminescence display at night that lasted a few weeks. These pictures were taken at La Jolla Shores. It was super dark, so much so, that I could not see the surfers and bathers until I looked at the pictures. I imagine it must be beautiful to be in the water surrounded by the bluish lights. Maybe I will try it in 2021.

June brings the beginning of beach season to Carlsbad in particlular and San Diego in general. The sunny skies and warmer water plus all those miles of coastline make San Diego County a great place to combat the isolation of the lockdowns.

Also in June: Fiona graduated from UCSD with honors. She received her Bachellor's in Cognitive Science. Although the 2020 grads missed out on the normal graduation ceremonies, they still got the satisfaction of a major achievement in their lives. Fiona is now working as a software developer at Solute, a Navy contractor. Way to go, Fiona!

Dinoflaagellates, are microscopic algae, which are responsible for the bioluminescence in the ocean.

During July and August, I spent the days taking hikes and going to the beach.

I need to include a sad announcement. In April, Fiona's grandfather and my former father in law, Walter Littman, passed away in a nursing home due to a COVID 19 infection. He was a Math professor at University of Minesotta where he taught many generations of students for 50 years. Sadly, like many other residents of nursing homes, he died in isolation due to the pandemic.

In the Fall, playing catch-up at work took a large portion of my time. With all the club closures and reopenings due to COVID, the team at 24 Hour Fitness had to scramble to keep up with all the changes that the business required. because of that, there were not a lot of outdoor activities.

Also, my little persimmon tree had quite a bit of delicious fruit this Fall.

In December, the houses around Carlsbad were beautifully decorated with Christmas lights. It was great fun to drive around the neighborhoods and enjoy the displays. I read somewhere that because of the pandemic and people spending more time at home, Christmas decorations took an added level of importance as a way to show that COVID would not dampen all holiday celebrations.

2020 was a tough year on every one of us.

Each of us struggled with different challenges but deep down we all know that things will begin to improve soon.

The end of the year has signified through many thousands of years a re-energizing renewal of nature. It is a pivot in time when the days begin to get longer and we see the darkness being pushed away, little by little, every day.

Just like that, we can envision a better time ahead where our lives will return to normalcy and we will be able to see those we love and do all the things we enjoy.

I hope your lives are happier and fulfilling in 2021.
