Valparaiso one of the most important ports in Chile. It is also a very old city full of history since it was founded by the Spanish conquistadors in the 1500s and later became a key stopping point in the trade routes around the Cape Horn. The city has a small flat section next to the ocean and the rest is built on the hills. People have learned to build on the hillsides and have constructed funiculars (called ascensores in Spanish) in every major hill in the city. The funiculars were built to help commuters navigate the steep hills. Additionally, there are stairs and narrow walkways throughout the hills. Valparaiso is full of murals painted to celebrate the arts and local culture.

The multi story building in the picture below is “La Sebastiana”, poet Pablo Neruda’s home in Valparaíso now a museum devoted to Neruda’s history. The cat in the picture is a resident at Neruda’s property.

Any exploration of the hills of Valparaiso starts and end with a ride on the Funicular. We went up the Espiritu Santo funicular and came down Cerro Artilleria.

Buenos Aires


Buenos Aires is more than just the capital of Argentina. It is also the most european city in South America. The buildings, parks, and the street layout can easily fool you into thinking you are in Spain or Italy. Buenos Aires is famous for its meats. Beef portions are so generous, it is hard to imagine finishing all that meat in just one meal. Downtown Buenos Aires is home to the Obelisk monument that commemorates Argentinian Independence, the Presidential Palace or Casa Rosada, the beautiful national cathedral that includes and the tomb of father of the country general José de San Martín. Farther north is the famous Recoleta Cemetery where illustrious  Argentinians are buried and the Ateneo bookstore a converted theater that now conforms one of the most beautiful bookstores in the world.

We also had the opportunity to attend a dinner tango show with extraordinary dancers.

In October, I also visited Guatemala including the city of Antigua, the Mayan pyramids of Tikal, and Guatemala City. Antigua is the original colonial capital of the country which was destroyed by two successive earthquakes and rebuilt before the government seat was moved to the current Guatemala City location.  The ruins of the old Cathedral, still standing after 250 years attest to the abandoned grandeur of the former capital. The town has many stores devoted to colorful ornaments and crafts. Former american school buses make up the bulk of the bus fleet in Guatemala. They are painted in bright colors with artistic designs and affectionately called “chicken buses”.

The trip to Guatemala also included a hike up the Pacaya volcano, a flight to the to the town of Flores and a ride into the jungle of Petén to visit the Mayan pyramids of Tikal in a jungle forest inhabited by a variety of animals including spider monkeys and coatis. Climbing the pyramids in the jungle heat was quite tiring. However, Tikal is a magnificent archeological site which deserves to be walked in its entirety.

The trip ended in Guatemala City with a visit to the beautiful government palace and the central market.

Guanajuato

Guanajuato, has the airport nearest to SMDA. It is a city in its own right with a long mining tradition and a major university. Guanajuato celebrates Dia de los Muertos differently by assigning stretches of the major street to institutions which use their small plots to produce beautiful sand and seeds paintings. The paintings depict different takes on the theme of death both as a cultural phenomenon or as a personal experience. The paintings are called “tapetes” or tapestries and are displayed throughout the festival until early Sunday morning. When the festival ends, the streets are cleaned and the beautiful ephemeral art disappears. The town has many distinctive buildings including the university of Guanajuato, the Teatro Juarez and several major colonial churches. It is also crisscrossed by a network of ancient tunnels built as a flood control works but now used for vehicle traffic.

San Miguel de Allende


Visiting San Miguel de Allende was a last minute decision. Halloween was coming and I realized that SMDA has one of the best celebrations of Dia de los Muertos in all of Mexico. Scores of people dress up in costumes and decorate themselves with “Catrina” (skull) make-up. There are make up artists in the streets with portable studios, and all the public buildings and businesses are decorated with flowers, garlands and altars. The celebration goes on all night with music and food. The main building in town is the Parroquia San Miguel Arcangel and is instantly recognizable by its beautiful orange spires.

Guadalajara and Tlaquepaque


Guadalajara is a town with a beautiful historical center with a magnificent cathedral, the imposing Teatro Degollado (lit up at night), and various government buildings from colonial times. There are enormous murals by José Clemente Orozco including the portrait of the Mexican emancipator Hidalgo.

Tlaquepaque is one of Mexico’s “magical towns”. It is full of displays of street art amd has the charm of a little town.
